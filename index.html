Three sample questions from the WEEDLAW curriculum. Education only — not legal advice.

1. What does Charter section 10(b) protect, and when must you assert it?

Charter s.10(b) — the right to retain and instruct counsel without delay and to be informed of that right — is triggered upon detention or arrest. You must be told of your right to a lawyer immediately. You must be given a reasonable opportunity to exercise that right before police questioning can begin.

Assert it clearly and unambiguously: "I want to speak with a lawyer. I am invoking my right to counsel under s.10(b) of the Charter." Failure by police to honor this right can result in evidence exclusion under s.24(2). The Weedlaw Education WEEDLAW STAND module covers this in full with scripts, case law, and application strategies.

2. What did R. v. Parker (2000) establish, and why is it foundational to cannabis law?

R. v. Parker, 2000 ONCA was a landmark Ontario Court of Appeal decision. Terrance Parker, an epilepsy patient who used cannabis to control seizures, challenged the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act (CDSA). The court found that the blanket prohibition on cannabis possession, as applied to someone with a medical necessity for cannabis, violated s.7 of the Charter (life, liberty, and security of the person).

The Court gave Parliament 12 months to create a medical exemption scheme — which led directly to the creation of the Marihuana Medical Access Regulations (MMAR) in 2001. Parker is foundational because it established that the constitutional right to health can override criminal drug law — a principle that has been built upon in nearly every major cannabis Charter case since.

3. What is the BENO-X Framework and how does it apply to a cannabis defence situation?

The BENO-X Framework is a constitutional analysis methodology developed by Derek Francisco (Doc Weedlaw) for evaluating cannabis legal situations through a Charter lens. It operates as a 3-step analysis chain:

Step 1 — Exemption Exists: Did the person hold a valid MMAR/ACMPR authorization at the time of the alleged offence? Documentation, licensing, and possession limits are analyzed.

Step 2 — Is It Functional? Was the exemption operationally valid — or was it rendered non-functional by a regulatory defect, administrative failure, or government-created gap? Cases like Sfetkopoulos v. Canada (2008 FC) are analyzed here.

Step 3 — Constitutional Consequence: If a valid exemption was blocked, what Charter remedy applies? Options include s.24(2) evidence exclusion, s.24(1) stay of proceedings, or acquittal.
